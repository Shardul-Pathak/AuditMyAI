# USER_INTERVIEWS.md

Two interviews conducted with potential users. Each 10–15 minutes. Both were informal conversations.

---

## Interview 1 — Friend, DevOps Intern

**Date:** 2025-05-07  
**Format:** Voice call  

**Background:**  
Intern working in a DevOps team at a start-up. Uses internal tooling, cloud dashboards, and limited AI tools for scripting and debugging support. Tool adoption is often bottom-up and fragmented across teams.

**Direct quotes:**

1. "We use a bunch of tools across teams, but nobody really knows what the full monthly cost is. It’s always split across accounts."

2. "Most AI tools come in through individuals first, then quietly spread. There’s no formal approval step unless it gets expensive."

3. "If I had something that showed overlap between tools doing the same job, that would immediately be useful. We waste time duplicating effort more than we waste money."

**Key observation:**  
Visibility of spend exists in fragments, but there is no consolidated view across tools and teams. Redundancy is more painful than cost.

**Implication:**  
Overlap detection and consolidation recommendations are higher value than pure cost tracking.

## Interview 2 — Senior College Student (Final Year, CS)

**Date:** 2025-05-10  
**Format:** Voice call  

**Background:**  
Final-year computer science student. Uses multiple AI and developer tools for coursework, internships, and personal projects. Tool usage is experimental and inconsistent, with no structured budget or tracking.

**Direct quotes:**

1. "When I start a project, I just try different tools until something works. I don’t really track what I’m already paying for."

2. "I’ve had cases where I was using two similar AI tools without realizing they overlap. I only notice when I’m low on credits or money."

3. "Cancelling something is harder than starting it. Even if I’m not using it much, I keep it just in case."

**Key observation:**  
Tool adoption is driven by experimentation, not cost awareness. Subscriptions persist due to inertia and perceived future usefulness.

**Implication:**  
The main value is surfacing inactive or redundant subscriptions with clear “safe to remove” signals, rather than general optimization advice.
